# Organic vs Paid: Understanding Your CAC by Channel

Calling organic traffic "free" is one of the most expensive mistakes a marketing executive can make.

That single word, free, has redirected billions in marketing spend toward channels that cost more, convert worse, and disappear the second you stop paying. Meanwhile, the channels that build compounding value sit underfunded because nobody bothered to calculate their actual cost.

Here's what the data shows: organic search customer acquisition cost runs 87.4% lower than paid search CAC according to **Terakeet** research from 2024. **First Page Sage** benchmarks put the average B2B organic CAC at $647 compared to $802 for paid search. And those gaps widen every year as ad auction competition drives paid costs higher while organic content compounds.

If you run marketing at a company spending more than $15,000 monthly on customer acquisition, you need to understand CAC by channel. Not blended. By channel. This article breaks down exactly how to calculate it, what benchmarks to use, and when each metric matters for different business decisions.

## The Hidden Cost of "Free" Traffic

The CFO who treats SEO as free traffic isn't saving money. They're flying blind on a channel that often drives 40-60% of website conversions.

This framing error leads to chronic underinvestment. Marketing teams get budget for paid media because those costs are visible and measurable. SEO gets whatever's left over because, well, it's free. The result? Companies pour money into channels with rising costs and linear returns while starving the channel with falling costs and compounding returns.

### Why Most Companies Get Organic CAC Wrong

Most marketing teams don't calculate organic CAC at all. When they do, they usually get it wrong in one of two ways.

The first mistake is treating organic as truly costless. Yes, you don't write a check to **Google** every time someone clicks. But someone wrote that content. Someone optimized those pages. Someone runs the technical infrastructure that makes ranking possible. Those costs exist whether you track them or not.

The second mistake is lumping all "inbound" together. Marketing qualified leads from organic search get mixed with referral traffic, direct visits, and branded queries. The organic CAC calculation gets polluted with channels that have completely different economics.

Both mistakes lead to the same outcome: bad budget allocation decisions based on incomplete data.

### What Counts as Organic Spend

Organic investment includes every cost required to acquire customers through non-paid search traffic. Here's what belongs in the calculation:

**Personnel costs**: Salaries and benefits for SEO managers, content writers, technical SEO specialists, and the percentage of developer time spent on SEO-related work. If your content marketing director spends 40% of their time on organic search initiatives, 40% of their total compensation goes into this bucket.

**Agency and contractor fees**: Retainers for SEO agencies, freelance writer payments, link building services, and technical SEO consultants. Include the full contract value, not just the hours specifically labeled as SEO.

**Content production**: Design costs for graphics and images, video production for embedded content, editing and proofreading, and any tools used specifically for content creation. Photography and stock image subscriptions count here too.

**Tools and technology**: **Ahrefs**, **Semrush**, **Screaming Frog**, **Surfer SEO**, **Clearscope**, rank tracking software, and any other paid tools used primarily for organic search work. If you use **HubSpot** or **Salesforce** for attribution tracking, include a proportional share.

The total of these costs, divided by customers attributed to organic search, gives you organic CAC. It's not complicated math. It's just math that most companies never do.

## How to Calculate CAC by Channel

Marketing executives need three CAC numbers: organic, paid, and blended. Each answers a different question, and using the wrong one leads to wrong decisions.

### The Organic CAC Formula

Organic CAC equals your total organic investment divided by customers attributed to organic search.

**Organic CAC = (SEO Salaries + Agency Fees + Content Costs + Tools) / Organic Customers**

The numerator captures everything from the previous section. The denominator requires attribution, which means your analytics setup needs to track which customers first found you through organic search and eventually converted.

For a B2B company spending $50,000 monthly on organic search efforts and acquiring 77 customers through that channel, organic CAC comes out to $649. That aligns with the $647 benchmark **First Page Sage** found for B2B thought leadership approaches.

The key distinction: organic CAC includes only the customers whose first meaningful touchpoint was organic search. If someone clicked a Google ad, then returned organically, then converted, that's a paid customer. The organic channel assisted, but didn't acquire.

### The Paid CAC Formula

Paid CAC follows the same structure but with different inputs.

**Paid CAC = (Media Spend + Agency Fees + Tools) / Paid Customers**

The big difference is media spend. Every dollar of ad budget goes directly into this calculation. There's no ambiguity about whether to include it.

For a company spending $80,000 monthly on **Google Ads** and **LinkedIn** advertising plus $15,000 in agency management fees, acquiring 100 customers through paid channels, paid CAC equals $950.

Notice what paid CAC doesn't include: the cost of landing pages, conversion rate optimization, or creative development. If you want the true cost, you'd add those in. Most companies don't, which makes their paid CAC look artificially low compared to organic.

### Blended CAC vs Channel-Specific CAC

Blended CAC divides total sales and marketing spend by all new customers, regardless of channel.

**Blended CAC = Total S&M Spend / All New Customers**

This number tells you overall acquisition efficiency. **OpenView Partners** found that blended CAC typically runs 20-40% lower than paid-only CAC because it includes the lower-cost organic customers in the denominator.

| CAC Type | Formula | What It Answers |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| Organic CAC | Organic Spend / Organic Customers | How efficient is our SEO investment? |
| Paid CAC | Paid Spend / Paid Customers | How efficient is our ad spend? |
| Blended CAC | Total S&M / All Customers | How efficient is overall acquisition? |

The distinction matters because blended CAC can mask channel-level problems. A company with $400 blended CAC might actually have $200 organic CAC and $700 paid CAC. If paid spend is increasing and organic investment stays flat, that blended number will rise even though the underlying channel metrics point to an obvious solution: invest more in organic.

## The Numbers That Matter

Theory doesn't allocate budget. Numbers do. Here's the data that makes the case for tracking CAC by channel.

### Organic vs Paid CAC Benchmarks

The research consistently shows organic beating paid on cost efficiency. But the magnitude surprises most executives.

**Terakeet's** 2024 analysis found organic search CAC running 87.4% lower than paid search CAC. That's not a marginal difference. That's organic acquiring customers at roughly one-eighth the cost of paid.

**First Page Sage's** 2025 benchmarks break it down by industry and approach:

| Channel | B2B CAC (Thought Leadership) | B2B CAC (Standard) |
|---------|------------------------------|--------------------|
| Organic Search | $647 | $942 |
| Paid Search | $802 | $802 |
| LinkedIn Organic | $658 | $658 |
| LinkedIn Paid | $983 | $983 |

The thought leadership approach, which means publishing expert-level content that builds authority, delivers organic CAC 19% lower than paid search. Even standard organic content beats paid by 15%.

For B2C companies, the gaps are similar. Retail organic CAC averages $68 versus $91 for paid search. Financial services sees $644 organic versus $728 paid.

### Why Organic CAC Falls While Paid CAC Rises

The trajectories matter more than the snapshots.

Paid CAC rises because ad auctions work against you. **WordStream's** 2024 analysis found Google Ads CPC increased for 86% of industries, with an average jump of 10% and some industries seeing 25% or higher. Cost per lead rose 25% year over year. **Search Engine Land** reported Facebook lead campaign CPL up 21% annually.

This isn't a bad year. It's the trend. **Focus Digital** found overall CAC increased 40-60% between 2023 and 2025. **Genesys Growth** tracked a 222% CAC surge over eight years in their client data.

Organic CAC falls because content compounds. The article you publish today ranks for 10 queries. Next year it ranks for 30 as it accumulates links and authority signals. The cost stays fixed while the customer acquisition increases.

**Digital Position** quantified this in their 2024 research: effective CPC for SEO drops to $0.59 at the 24-month mark compared to $2.51 for paid search. That's a 328% efficiency advantage that simply doesn't exist in month one.

### Conversion Rate Differences

CAC tells part of the story. Conversion rate tells the rest.

**SagaPixel's** 2024 benchmark data shows organic search converting at 2.4% versus 1.3% for PPC. Organic leads convert nearly twice as often as paid leads.

The gap widens when you look at inbound versus outbound. Organic inbound leads convert at 14.6% compared to 1.7% for traditional outbound methods. People who find you through search already have intent. They're looking for what you sell. Paid traffic interrupts people who may or may not care.

Higher conversion rates mean fewer leads needed to hit revenue targets. Fewer leads needed means lower CAC. The math compounds.

## The Compounding Effect

The cost difference between organic and paid comes down to a simple distinction: one is an expense, the other is an asset.

### Paid Spend: Linear Returns

Paid media delivers value in direct proportion to spend. Put in $1, get some fraction of a customer. Put in $10,000, get proportionally more customers. Stop spending, get nothing.

This isn't a criticism. Paid has legitimate uses, especially for immediate revenue needs, testing messages, and scaling quickly when unit economics work. But the returns are fundamentally linear.

Worse, they're linear with rising costs. That $10,000 bought more customers last year than it will this year. Next year it'll buy fewer still. The auction never stops.

If you stop advertising tomorrow, your paid acquisition goes to zero within days. There's no residual value from the money you already spent. The clicks are gone. The impressions are gone. You rented attention rather than building ownership.

### Organic Investment: Compounding Returns

Organic content behaves like an appreciating asset. The money you spend today creates value that grows over time.

A well-optimized article published in January starts ranking for its target query by April. By August, it's picking up long-tail variations. By the following January, it's accumulated backlinks and social signals that help it rank for queries you never specifically targeted.

The cost was fixed at publication. The returns compound indefinitely.

**SeoProfy** cited **Google** research showing SEO ROI at 5.3x versus 2x for paid search. Some analyses put it higher. **NikshDigital** found $22 returned for every $1 spent on SEO versus $2 for paid.

The 70% of marketers who say SEO generates more sales than PPC aren't wrong. They're observing what happens when you compare a depreciating expense to an appreciating asset.

### The 24-Month Crossover Point

Most executives want to know: how long until SEO pays off?

The honest answer is 6-12 months for positive ROI. That's when cumulative organic revenue exceeds cumulative organic investment. But that's the wrong question.

The better question is: when does organic become dramatically more efficient than paid?

The answer is around 24 months. **Digital Position's** research shows effective CPC for SEO at $0.59 after two years versus $2.51 for paid. At that point, organic delivers more than 4x the traffic per dollar.

This creates a paradox for short-term focused executives. Paid looks better in month one because organic hasn't matured. But companies that judge channels on 90-day windows systematically underinvest in organic and overpay for customers through paid.

The companies winning on CAC efficiency are the ones who made organic investments 24 months ago. The best time to start was two years ago. The second best time is now.

## When to Use Each Metric

Three CAC numbers means three different contexts for using them. Getting this wrong leads to bad presentations and worse decisions.

### Board Reporting: Use Blended CAC

Boards want to know overall acquisition efficiency. They're comparing your company to benchmarks, evaluating path to profitability, and judging management performance.

Blended CAC serves this purpose. It shows total investment relative to total new customers without requiring the board to understand channel-level attribution complexities.

The LTV:CAC ratio works the same way. A healthy benchmark is 3:1, meaning customer lifetime value should exceed acquisition cost by at least three times. Report this at the blended level for governance purposes.

Keep channel-level data in the appendix. Bring it up when the board asks about specific growth initiatives or when explaining material changes in the blended number.

### Budget Allocation: Use Channel CAC

When deciding where to invest the next marketing dollar, blended CAC is useless. It tells you nothing about which channel offers the best marginal return.

Channel-specific CAC answers the allocation question directly. If organic CAC is $647 and paid CAC is $802, and both channels have room to scale, the next dollar should go toward organic.

But the analysis goes deeper. You need to know:

- **CAC trend by channel**: Is organic CAC falling as content compounds? Is paid CAC rising with auction competition?
- **Capacity by channel**: Can you actually spend more on organic? Do you have content production bandwidth?
- **LTV by channel**: Do organic customers have higher lifetime value than paid customers?

Most companies find organic customers have 15-25% higher LTV because search intent correlates with purchase readiness. That makes the CAC gap even more significant on a unit economics basis.

### The Spend Threshold Rule

Not every company needs channel-specific CAC tracking. The complexity has a cost, and that cost isn't worth it at smaller scales.

The threshold sits around $15-20k monthly in marketing spend.

Below $15k/month, blended CAC is sufficient. Attribution complexity isn't worth the analytics investment. The potential efficiency gains from optimizing channel mix are too small to move the needle.

Above $20k/month, channel-specific CAC becomes critical. A 10% efficiency improvement on a $20k monthly spend saves $24,000 annually. That's real money. It justifies the analytics infrastructure to track it properly.

| Monthly Spend | Recommended Approach | Reason |
|---------------|---------------------|--------|
| Under $15k | Blended CAC only | Complexity cost exceeds benefit |
| $15k-$20k | Transition period | Begin building attribution infrastructure |
| Over $20k | Channel-specific CAC | 10% gains = significant runway |

## Common Objections

Marketing executives hear the same pushback when proposing organic investment. Here's how to address it.

### "We Can't Attribute Organic Conversions"

This objection made sense in 2015. It doesn't in 2025.

**Google Analytics 4** tracks organic search as a traffic source by default. The data exists. The question is whether your conversion tracking captures the full customer journey.

For most B2B companies, the gap is between website conversion and CRM closed-won. The fix requires connecting GA4 to your CRM through UTM parameters, hidden form fields, or direct API integration.

**HubSpot**, **Salesforce**, and most modern CRMs support this connection natively. The technical lift is days or weeks of work, not months. If your analytics can't answer "how many closed deals came from organic search," that's a solvable problem, not an inherent limitation of the channel.

Attribution will never be perfect. But imperfect attribution is better than no attribution, and "we can't measure it" usually means "we haven't tried."

### "SEO Takes Too Long to Show ROI"

The 6-12 month timeline for positive ROI is real. But the framing is wrong.

Paid campaigns also take time to optimize. The first month of any paid program involves testing audiences, refining creative, and learning what works. The "immediate ROI" pitch for paid media assumes you start with perfect targeting. Nobody does.

More importantly, the long-term trajectory matters more than the short-term starting point. At 24 months, organic delivers a 328% advantage in effective CPC. The company that waited for "faster" results from paid will have spent more money acquiring fewer customers.

The real question is whether your company plans to exist in 24 months. If yes, organic investment pays off. If you're optimizing for a 90-day exit, sure, paid is faster. Most businesses aren't in that situation.

### "Paid Is More Scalable"

This objection confuses scalability with simplicity.

Paid scales with spend. Double the budget, double the traffic (roughly, until you hit audience saturation). The mechanics are straightforward.

Organic scales with authority. More content, more backlinks, more topical coverage, more queries ranked. The mechanics are different, not worse.

The scalability question really asks: can we grow organic at the same rate as paid?

For most companies under $50M revenue, yes. There's more content to create, more pages to optimize, more authority to build. The constraint isn't the channel. It's the investment.

Companies at scale, with thousands of pages already ranking, do hit organic ceilings. But they've also built an asset generating millions in value annually. The "scalability" problem is actually a success story.

## Building Your CAC Dashboard

Knowing CAC by channel matters only if you track it consistently. Here's the reporting infrastructure that makes it operational.

### Metrics to Track Monthly

**Primary CAC metrics**:
- Blended CAC (total S&M spend divided by all new customers)
- Organic CAC (organic investment divided by organic-attributed customers)
- Paid CAC (paid spend divided by paid-attributed customers)
- CAC by channel trend (3-month rolling average to smooth volatility)

**Supporting metrics**:
- LTV:CAC by channel (not just blended; organic often exceeds 5:1)
- CAC payback period by channel (months to recover acquisition cost)
- Conversion rate by channel (organic typically 1.5-2x higher)
- Cost per lead by channel (leading indicator of CAC trends)

**Attribution health**:
- Percentage of customers with attributed source (target 85%+)
- Organic vs paid vs direct/unknown split
- Multi-touch attribution if running sophisticated programs

Build this dashboard in whatever tool you already use for reporting. **Looker**, **Tableau**, **Google Sheets** connected to your CRM, or even a monthly slide deck works. The format matters less than the consistency of tracking.

### Setting Channel-Specific Targets

Targets should reflect the compounding nature of organic investment.

Organic CAC should decrease 10-15% year over year as content matures. If it's rising, something is wrong: content isn't ranking, production costs are bloating, or attribution is broken.

Paid CAC will likely increase 5-15% annually due to auction dynamics. Budget for this. If paid CAC holds flat, you're either finding new efficiency or avoiding competitive auctions. If it's rising faster than 15%, investigate whether paid still makes sense at current scale.

Blended CAC target depends on your channel mix. Heavy organic mix means blended should fall modestly each year. Heavy paid mix means blended will rise unless organic investment increases.

| Metric | Target Trajectory | Red Flag |
|--------|------------------|----------|
| Organic CAC | -10 to -15% YoY | Rising year over year |
| Paid CAC | +5 to +15% YoY | Rising faster than 15% |
| Blended CAC | Flat to -5% YoY | Rising faster than paid inflation |
| LTV:CAC (Organic) | >4:1 | Below 3:1 |
| LTV:CAC (Paid) | >3:1 | Below 2:1 |
